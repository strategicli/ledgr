# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

One user: Brandon Collins, Executive Pastor at Edgewood Community Church. Ledgr is his personal life-management system, the daily home for his meetings, tasks, notes, links, and the people he works with. He is a power user of his own tool and spends real hours inside it, often switching between a phone on the move and a desktop when he sits down to work. The job to be done is to run his whole working life from one place he trusts, replacing Notion, without touching a spreadsheet or a second app.

The architecture is single-user by design but multi-user-ready: every item is owner-scoped, and the visual layer is meant to be skinnable so a future second user (Tyler runs his own instance) can carry a different palette and vibe from their own settings. The theme this pass designs is "Brandon's feel"; treat color and mood as owner-configurable variables, not hardcoded brand law.

## Product Purpose

Ledgr replaces a sprawl of Notion databases with one typed-item system: everything (tasks, meetings, notes, links, people, and bespoke types like sermons or songs) is a row in a single items table, presented through two surfaces. Work is the daily-use side (glanceable, mobile-friendly, a configurable bar of the user's destinations); Build is where the system itself is configured and maintained (deliberate, desktop-first). Success is that Ledgr is the place Brandon reaches for first every day and is glad to be in, that it holds up offline on a Sunday, and that it stays fast for him and cheap to run.

## Positioning

A personal life system that is bespoke-first, not generic-first: each kind of content gets a purpose-built shape rather than a blank database, so the tool fits the work instead of the work bending to fit the tool.

## Brand Personality

Ledgr should feel like a place worth spending hours in, not a utility you tolerate. Fluid and thumb-friendly on mobile, powerful and dense on desktop, with one unified identity across both so it never feels like two apps stitched together. Dense and fast for a fluent power user (information-rich, keyboard-reachable, rewards familiarity) but warm and welcoming where it can be, because it is one person's life, not an enterprise console. Editorial in its typography and hierarchy: considered, confident, reads like a well-set document. Attractive on purpose. The voice in any generated prose is warm, direct, concise, active, and never uses em dashes.

## Anti-references

Not a generic SaaS dashboard: no gradient hero-metric tiles, no endless identical icon-heading-text card grids, none of the AI-slop template look. Not corporate or enterprise: no sterile heavy-chrome gray, no dense toolbar walls. Not a Notion re-skin: Ledgr is bespoke-first, so it should not read as generic-database-with-a-coat-of-paint.

## Design Principles

Fast for the user, cheap on the back end: every visual choice respects optimistic UI, lazy loading, and virtualized lists, and never buys polish with a performance cost the user feels.

One identity, two surfaces: Work and Build look and feel like the same product; the difference is posture (glanceable vs. deliberate, mobile-first vs. desktop-first), not two visual languages.

Bespoke-first, legible always: purpose-built shapes over generic databases, but every bespoke control earns a clear label or affordance; nothing ships unexplained.

Themeable by design: color and mood are owner-configurable, so the visual system is built as tokens and roles a user can re-skin, not fixed brand constants.

Welcoming and efficient at once: dense enough for a power user to move fast, warm enough that spending hours here feels good rather than draining.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Single-user tool, so keep accessibility to sensible basics rather than a full compliance program: readable contrast (favor the ink end of the ramp over elegant light grays, a past pain point), visible focus states, and honored reduced-motion. No investment in machinery beyond what one user actually benefits from.
