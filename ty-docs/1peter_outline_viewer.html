Introduction — Paper Opener
The Question Every Believer Faces
First Peter is a powerful letter because it addresses what every Christian must face: How do I live in a world that is hostile to me? How should Christians live as elect exiles? Peter deals with this very real tension — we are chosen, but to be an exile means that you are not in your home; you look forward to a better time. To this people that faced reviling for bearing the name of Christ, Peter speaks instruction, hope, and the example of Christ who gave His life for us. The letter, then, is a call to stand fast in the midst of persecution — to suffer well, live honorably, submit, and hold fast to a future hope. This elect exile identity becomes the lens through which everything in the letter is read; it is introduced here and returned to at the close.
"It is a letter written to encourage a slandered and marginalized minority living in the midst of the Roman Empire."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 148
"The Apostle Peter, the one on whom Jesus said he would build his church, writes to those dispersed throughout the Roman Empire in modern-day Turkey, arguing that they are elect exiles and therefore they have hope."

Schreiner, "1 Peter Intro"
"To God, those in Asia Minor are chosen, but to the world, they are exiles and sojourners. This becomes the theme of the letter."

Authorship
Who Wrote It?
Argue for Petrine authorship. Skepticism is a modern phenomenon — the early church was nearly unanimous. Eusebius listed 1 Peter as the only "undisputed" General Epistle.
"Doubt about 1 Peter is, however, a rather modern phenomenon. In the early church few books enjoyed the confidence the church displayed for 1 Peter."
KKQ, 830
"The early tradition of the church was thoroughly acquainted with 1 Peter and attributed authorship of the book to the apostle Peter in an impressive way."
KKQ, 832
"Both Clement of Alexandria and Tertullian (ca. AD 160–225) cited 1 Peter and attributed the verses to the apostle. Eusebius, in fact, listed 1 Peter as the only 'undisputed' book of all the General Epistles."
KKQ, 832
Provenance
Where Was It Written?
"Babylon" in 5:13 is best understood as a metaphor for Rome — the center of Gentile power. Mesopotamian Babylon was deserted; Egyptian Babylon was an insignificant outpost. Rome is the clear best option.
"First Peter specifically mentions 'Babylon' in 5:13 as the place from which the letter was sent. Three options arise from this description. First, the location could be Mesopotamian Babylon. However, at this time the city was all but deserted. Second, there was a Babylon in Egypt, but it was an insignificant military outpost… The third and best option is Rome. In this case the term is metaphorical, designating the center of Gentile power."
KKQ, 839
Destination
Who Were the Recipients?
Believers scattered across northern Asia Minor (modern Turkey) — a crescent-shaped region. The terms "exiles" and "dispersed" refer to believers in general, not specifically to Jewish Christians, as most modern scholars understand.
"First Peter 1:1 identifies where the recipients of the letter lived: northern Asia Minor (modern Turkey). Peter listed a series of Roman provinces in an unusual order, 'to those chosen, living as exiles dispersed abroad in Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia' (1:1). This would include a crescent-shaped region of northern Asia Minor."
KKQ, 840
"Most modern interpreters understand these terms to be metaphorical… [referring to believers generally, not specifically to the Jewish diaspora]."
KKQ, 840
Occasion
Why Was It Written?
The occasion is persecution — real but not yet lethal. These believers were suffering socially, relationally, and locally. Walk through the specific forms of suffering Peter names. They were not yet being executed as criminals.
"Persecution is a common theme in 1 Peter and serves as the occasion for the writing of this letter. While Peter may have been anticipating the persecution of Nero in Rome, his readers were most likely already experiencing private persecution or some localized state persecution."
KKQ, 840
"They suffered from various trials (1:6); endured grief from suffering unjustly (2:19); were accused and their Christian life denounced (3:16); were slandered (4:4); suffered fiery ordeals (4:12); shared in the sufferings of the Messiah (4:13); were ridiculed for the name of Christ (4:14); and suffered according to God's will (4:19). But they were not (yet) being executed as criminals."
KKQ, 840
Purpose
What Was Peter Trying to Do?
Peter promotes a biblical worldview — identity before ethics. Before telling them how to live, he tells them who they are. They must understand whose they are and then face their situation from that vantage point.
"Persecution was not an abstract notion for the believers in Asia Minor; they were undergoing fierce repercussions for their faith. Peter encouraged Christians to endure in the face of difficult times. He did this by promoting a biblical worldview among the believers. They needed to understand who (or whose) they were and then face their situation from this vantage point."
KKQ, 841
"The essence of this exhortation is in 1:5–6: '[You] are being guarded by God's power through faith for a salvation that is ready to be revealed in the last time. You rejoice in this, even though now for a short time, if necessary, you experience grief in various trials.'"
KKQ, 841
Paragraph 1
Gospel Foundation
1:1–2:10
Before Peter tells them how to live, he tells them who they are. Identity precedes ethics. The opening section grounds everything that follows in the gospel: they have been born again to a living hope, an imperishable inheritance, and a future salvation that cannot be taken away. Peter encouraged Christians to endure by promoting a biblical worldview — they needed to understand who, or whose, they were before they could face their situation faithfully.
"Their new birth has given them a living hope, an inheritance, a future salvation that can never be taken away."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 150
"They needed to understand who (or whose) they were and then face their situation from this vantage point."
KKQ, 841
"[You] are being guarded by God's power through faith for a salvation that is ready to be revealed in the last time. You rejoice in this, even though now for a short time, if necessary, you experience grief in various trials." (1:5–6)
KKQ, 841
Paragraph 2
Ethics of Exile
2:11–3:12
With identity established, Peter turns to conduct. Elect exiles are to not withdraw but to engage the world honorably. Peter calls them to resist sinful practices and to have honorable, virtuous, and good conduct — what Schreiner calls kalos, good in a good and beautiful and blameless and excellent way, recognizable as good even to unbelievers. This conduct is largely defined by submission: to government, to masters, within marriage. Submission is not weakness — it is the exile's calling, modeled by Christ himself. Central to this section is Christ as the faithful sufferer who did not retaliate, and whose body was their substitute.
"After encouraging Gentiles concerning their new hope, Peter turns to how to live as exiles in the midst of a pagan society. Essentially, he calls them to resist sinful practices and to have honorable, virtuous, and good conduct."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 151
"These good works are largely identified as submission rather than retaliation, perseverance rather than vacillation."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 151
"Positively, they are to not withdraw but conduct themselves properly. This word here is kalos in Greek — good, good in a good and beautiful and blameless and excellent way. Peter expects that his readers live in a way that will be recognized as good even by the standards of unbelievers."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"Central to this section is the example of Christ as the 'faithful slave and citizen' who subjugated Himself to unjust authorities (see 2:21–25). Not only is He an example, but His sacrificial body was their substitute."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 151
"Keeping their exile identity on this earth means looking to Christ's example of suffering and doing good even to those who mistreat them."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 151
Paragraph 3
Suffering and Vindication
3:13–4:11
Suffering is not random — it is purposeful, shared, and temporary. Peter frames hardship not as abandonment but as a purifying fire. To be disgraced is an honor; to be insulted is to be blessed. Their response should be joy, not despair, because their suffering is participation in the sufferings of Christ. He calls them: "To this you have been called, because Christ also suffered for you, leaving you an example, so that you might follow in his steps" (1 Pet. 2:21). Their vindication is coming when God visits.
"Their hardship is a purifying and proving fire. Their response should be joy because in the midst of suffering they are blessed."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 152
"He tells them that to be disgraced is an honor, to be insulted is to be blessed, and he bases this all on the life of Jesus Christ."
Schreiner, "1 Peter Intro"
"Though they will be slandered as evildoers, their good works will justify them on the last day when God visits."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
Paragraph 4
The Church Under Pressure
4:12–5:11
The letter closes with instruction for the community under pressure, especially its leaders. Elders are to shepherd the flock with pure motives and be a good example — but they must remember they are simply undershepherds. Christ is the true Shepherd who will lead this people out of the valley of the shadow of death. The whole congregation is called to humility and alertness against their true enemy: not the Roman Empire, but the devil, who prowls like a roaring lion. The call to stand fast is a community call, not just an individual one.
"Leaders in the congregation are to care for the flock as shepherds, following the example of Christ. All of them are to be humble and alert, taking their stand against their true enemy, the devil."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 152
"Elders need to help their flock through this trying time. They are to shepherd them with pure motives and be a good example to those in their care. They also must remember they are simply undershepherds: Christ is the true Shepherd who will lead this people out of the valley of the shadow of death."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 153
App 1
Submission, Sovereignty, and Loyalty to God
Trust God's hand even in corrupt or unjust authorities. Our ultimate loyalty belongs to God alone — not any party, politician, or nation. Submission may mean suffering. We engage, we submit, we work for the good of society — but we do not find our identity in the governmental system. We are citizens of another country first.
"Peter calls them to submit to governing authorities, to respect and honor them, but also to not rely on them, to not find our identity in the governmental system."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"What this means is that loyalty to Christ does not equal rebellion against the government."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"The government is not your ultimate place of loyalty. No politician, no political party is your ultimate place of loyalty — only God himself and his people."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"Remember, the emperor at times required worship, and Peter says you are free because you serve God; he is your master. So in submitting to them, you're actually declaring your freedom in God."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
App 2
Human Flourishing and Common Grace
God cares about human civilization and uses secular institutions to accomplish good in the world. The creation mandate (Gen 1) was never rescinded — we are called to build human civilization for flourishing and to share the gospel. Peter's call to do public good flows from this. Christians should be the first to joyfully support society for the benefit of all humanity.
"God blesses the world consistently through non-Christian institutions. He does so through families, schools, businesses, and we should be the first as Christians to joyfully support such things for the benefit of humanity."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"Peter says Christians should be characterized as those who do public good. This is part of our witness."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"In other words, governing authorities order society; they enact justice, they pursue peace in society. God uses politicians to do good."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"Martin Luther, who has a way with words, said it this way: Christians are like manure — when they are gathered all in one place, they stink, but when they spread out, they do some good."
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
App 3
Living for Another World
This is not our home. Peter calls believers strangers and exiles as a defining identity. Connect to Heb 11:13–16 — the patriarchs as sojourners looking for a better country whose builder and maker is God. All of the letter's ethics flow from this: we are living toward something better. Close on hope.
"Peter's call in his letters is to suffer well, to live honorably among those with whom you don't agree, to press on to holiness, and to remember the hope that is coming."
Schreiner, "1 Peter Intro"
"Here he tells them to have a visitor or exile mindset here on this earth. They are strangers, they are exiles. As strangers, they are to be good guests…"
Schreiner, "1 Peter" video
"Their new birth has given them a living hope, an inheritance, a future salvation that can never be taken away."
Schreiner, The Visual Word, 150
